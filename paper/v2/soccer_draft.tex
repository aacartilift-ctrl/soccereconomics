\begin{abstract}
\noindent
In November 2022, FIFA instructed match officials to enforce the
long-standing but under-applied requirement that referees fully
compensate for time lost during professional football matches. Using
difference-in-differences across 15~European leagues and over
36,000~matches, we find that treated leagues added 0.74~minutes of
second-half stoppage time relative to never-treated controls ($p =
0.007$; wild cluster bootstrap $p = 0.036$). Compliance was
near-universal: 95--100\% of referees individually increased their
added time, with the coefficient of variation across referees
declining from 0.173 to 0.144. Total goals per match were
unchanged ($\beta = -0.033$, $p = 0.39$); goals were displaced
into stoppage time, not created. Per-minute scoring rates held
constant, and 87\% of the late-goal increase reflects additional
minutes rather than heightened intensity---a pure quantity effect.
The share of matches in which the result changed during stoppage
time rose from 7.3\% to 10.3\%, adding approximately 55~decisive
moments per Big~Five season. The directive achieved near-universal
compliance through focal-point coordination rather than monitoring,
fines, or sanctions.

\end{abstract}
