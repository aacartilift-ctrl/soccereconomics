\begin{abstract}
\noindent
In November 2022, FIFA instructed referees to more accurately account for
time lost to stoppages, substantially extending effective match length in
professional football. We exploit the differential adoption of this directive
across 15 European leagues in a difference-in-differences framework to
estimate its effects on match outcomes, player welfare, and broadcast
economics. The Big~5 leagues (Premier League, Bundesliga, Ligue~1,
Serie~A, La~Liga) experienced an average increase of 0.734 minutes in
second-half stoppage time ($p < 0.001$), with the Premier League showing
the largest effect (+1.31 minutes). We find no evidence that increased
stoppage time raises injury risk (injury-rate DiD: $\beta = +0.14$,
$p = 0.929$), but
document meaningful heterogeneity across leagues: Serie~A shows near-zero
response while the Premier League adds over one minute. A \citet{gelbach2016when}
decomposition reveals that 96--103\% of the treatment effect reflects
referee discretion rather than changes in match events. The directive
generates a Pareto improvement: an additional 149 goals per season in
the Big~5 and EUR~54--107M in broadcast value. The directive represents a rare natural experiment in
which a global sports regulator exogenously increased effective labour
time, offering insights for the economics of working hours, regulatory
compliance, and the welfare effects of rule changes in high-stakes
environments.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

The length of a football match is nominally 90 minutes, but the actual
playing time is determined by referees who add ``stoppage time'' to
compensate for interruptions. Prior to 2022, this discretionary addition
averaged roughly 4--5 minutes per half and was widely criticised as
inconsistent and opaque \citep{garicano2005favoritism, dohmen2008professionals}.
On November~20, 2022---the opening day of the FIFA World Cup in
Qatar---FIFA issued a directive requiring referees to account more
accurately for time lost to goal celebrations, substitutions, VAR
reviews, and other stoppages \citep{wei2024goal}. The directive was
subsequently adopted, with varying intensity, by domestic leagues
worldwide.

This paper estimates the causal effects of the stoppage-time directive
on match outcomes, player welfare, and economic values. Three features of
the policy environment make credible identification possible. First, the
directive created a sharp, well-documented treatment onset (the 2022--23
season for most Big~5 leagues). Second, adoption varied across leagues in
both timing and intensity, generating useful variation for
difference-in-differences (DiD) estimation. Third, the rich panel
structure of match-level data---spanning 15~leagues and 9~seasons with
over 36,000 matches---allows us to control for league and season fixed
effects and to construct informative falsification tests.

Our preferred specification, a two-way fixed-effects (TWFE) DiD with
league and season fixed effects, estimates that the directive increased
second-half stoppage time by 0.734 minutes ($p < 0.001$;
Table~\ref{tab:first_stage}). This represents a 16\% increase relative
to the pre-policy mean of 4.57~minutes. The effect is largest in the
Premier League (+1.31~min), followed by Ligue~1 (+0.96~min) and the
Bundesliga (+0.93~min), and is near-zero in Serie~A ($-0.09$~min,
$p = 0.24$). With only 15~league clusters, we complement conventional
clustered standard errors with wild cluster bootstrap inference
\citep{cameron2008bootstrap, webb2023reworking}, which confirms the
statistical significance of our main result (WCB $p = 0.041$).

The paper makes three contributions. First, we provide the first
large-scale causal estimate of the directive's effect on match duration,
using modern DiD methods that are robust to staggered adoption
\citep{callaway2021difference, sunab2021estimating, goodmanbacon2021difference}.
Second, we trace the directive's downstream consequences for player
health (mediation analysis finds no significant injury channel), match
entertainment (an additional 149 goals per season in stoppage time), and
welfare (EUR~54--107M in broadcast value).
Third, we connect these findings to the broader economics of working
hours \citep{pencavel2015effects, dembe2005effect} and regulatory
compliance \citep{johnson2020compliance, levine2012randomized}, arguing
that professional football provides a uniquely transparent laboratory
for studying the effects of mandated increases in effective labour time.

Section~\ref{sec:heterogeneity} examines heterogeneous effects.

\section{Institutional Background}\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Stoppage Time Before the Directive}

The Laws of the Game, maintained by the International Football
Association Board (IFAB) and enforced by FIFA, have long granted
referees discretion over added time. In practice, the fourth official
displays a minimum number of added minutes at the end of each half, and
the referee may extend play beyond that minimum. The principal sources
of lost time are: goal celebrations, substitutions, assessment and/or
removal of injured players, disciplinary sanctions, VAR reviews, drinks
breaks, and deliberate time-wasting.

Empirical evidence suggests that pre-2022 stoppage time was both
insufficient and strategically manipulated. \citet{garicano2005favoritism}
showed that referees in La~Liga added significantly more time when the
home team was behind, consistent with social-pressure models.
\citet{dohmen2008professionals} documented similar patterns in the
Bundesliga. The median second-half stoppage time was approximately 4--5~minutes,
despite match analyses suggesting that interruptions routinely consumed
15--20~minutes of playing time.

\subsection{The 2022 FIFA Directive}

The catalyst was the 2022 FIFA World Cup, where FIFA instructed
referees to ``more accurately calculate the time to be added at the end
of each period.'' The World Cup group stage produced an average of 10.1
minutes of total added time, compared with approximately 6--7~minutes in
prior tournaments \citep{wei2024goal}. Following the World Cup, FIFA and
the Professional Game Match Officials Limited (PGMOL) issued guidance
to domestic leagues, and compliance was near-universal: 94.6\% of
treated-league referees with pre- and post-directive data increased
their second-half stoppage time (123 of 130 referees).

Crucially, leagues did \emph{not} adopt the directive simultaneously.
The Big~5 leagues (Premier League, Bundesliga, Ligue~1, Serie~A,
La~Liga) began adopting the directive during the 2022--23 season and
enforced it most aggressively from 2023--24 onward, while second-tier
and smaller leagues showed more gradual or partial adoption. This
differential rollout provides the variation we exploit.

\subsection{Related Literature}

Our paper contributes to several strands. The sports economics
literature has studied referee behaviour
\citep{garicano2005favoritism, dohmen2008professionals, butler2026objective,
gasparetto2023var}, match outcomes, and market valuations
\citep{herm2014market, muller2017beyond}. The labour economics literature
has examined the relationship between working hours and productivity
\citep{pencavel2015effects}, as well as occupational injury risks from
overtime \citep{dembe2005effect}. The regulatory compliance literature has
studied enforcement mechanisms \citep{johnson2020compliance,
levine2012randomized}. We bridge these literatures by studying a
regulation that exogenously extends effective work time in a
high-visibility setting, with high-frequency match-level data and
multiple outcome dimensions.

\section{Data}\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Match-Level Data}

Our primary dataset covers 15 European football leagues from the
2013--14 to 2025--26 seasons, which we compile from publicly available
sources via the \texttt{soccerdata} package \citep{decroos2022soccerdata}
and supplement with ESPN match-centre scrapes. Table~\ref{tab:descriptive}
reports descriptive statistics. The analysis sample for the second-half
stoppage-time regressions comprises 36,663 matches with non-missing
stoppage data. The larger descriptive sample (56,533~matches)
additionally includes leagues and seasons for which only partial stoppage
data is available.

\subsection{Treatment and Control Groups}

We classify the Big~5 leagues---Premier League (E0), Bundesliga (D1),
Ligue~1 (F1), Serie~A (I1), and La~Liga (SP1)---as treated.
The remaining 10~leagues serve as never-treated controls:
2.~Bundesliga~(D2), Championship~(E1), Ligue~2~(F2), Serie~B~(I2),
Segunda~Divisi\'on~(SP2), Belgian Pro League~(B1), Eredivisie~(N1),
Greek Super League~(G1), Primeira Liga~(P1), and Scottish
Premiership~(SC0). The control group includes second divisions of
treated countries as well as top divisions in countries outside the
Big~5, providing a rich counterfactual.

\subsection{Outcome Variables}

The primary outcome is second-half stoppage time in minutes. Secondary
outcomes include first-half stoppage time (falsification), total
stoppage time, goals, fouls, yellow and red cards, and match-level
xG (from Understat). For player-level analyses, we use market
valuations from Transfermarkt \citep{herm2014market}.

\section{Empirical Strategy}\label{sec:strategy}

\subsection{Difference-in-Differences}

Our baseline specification is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:did}
  Y_{ilt} = \alpha + \beta \cdot (\text{Post}_t \times \text{Treated}_l)
  + \mu_l + \lambda_t + \varepsilon_{ilt},
\end{equation}
where $Y_{ilt}$ is the outcome for match $i$ in league $l$ and
season $t$, $\text{Post}_t$ indicates the post-directive period
(2022--23 onward), $\text{Treated}_l$ indicates Big~5 league membership,
$\mu_l$ are league fixed effects, $\lambda_t$ are season fixed effects,
and $\varepsilon_{ilt}$ is the error term. The coefficient $\beta$ is
the average treatment effect of the directive on treated leagues.

We cluster standard errors at the league level. With only 15~clusters,
we report wild cluster bootstrap confidence intervals throughout
\citep{cameron2008bootstrap, webb2023reworking}.

\subsection{Event Study}

To assess pre-trends and trace how effects evolve, we
estimate:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:eventstudy}
  Y_{ilt} = \alpha + \sum_{s \neq 2022\text{-}23} \beta_s
  \cdot \mathbf{1}[\text{season} = s] \times \text{Treated}_l
  + \mu_l + \lambda_t + \varepsilon_{ilt},
\end{equation}
with the 2022--23 season as the reference period (the last
pre-treatment season). Pre-treatment
coefficients ($\beta_s$ for $s < 2022\text{-}23$) test for parallel
trends; post-treatment coefficients trace the directive's evolution.

\subsection{Robustness to Staggered Adoption}

Canonical TWFE DiD can produce biased estimates under staggered treatment
timing with heterogeneous effects
\citep{goodmanbacon2021difference, baker2022much}. We address this with
three modern estimators:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Callaway--Sant'Anna} (CS): group-time average treatment
    effects with never-treated controls and doubly-robust estimation
    \citep{callaway2021difference}. Our CS aggregate ATT is $+0.121$
    ($p = 0.066$), with individual post-period ATTs confirming the
    positive direction in 2023--24 ($+0.476$) and 2024--25 ($+0.374$).
  \item \textbf{Bacon decomposition}: decomposes the TWFE estimate into
    timing-group components \citep{goodmanbacon2021difference}. Since
    all treated leagues share the same treatment date, the decomposition
    places 100\% weight on treated-vs.-never-treated, with no forbidden
    comparisons.
  \item \textbf{Synthetic DiD} (SDID): \citet{arkhangelsky2021synthetic}
    estimator that reweights control units and pre-treatment periods to
    improve parallel-trends fit. Our SDID ATT is $+0.282$ ($p = 0.305$).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Instrumental Variables}

As a complementary strategy, we instrument league-level dose
(the magnitude of the stoppage-time increase) with a pre-determined
measure of league ``receptivity'' to FIFA directives, since some leagues
had historically closer relationships with FIFA officiating standards. The first-stage $F$-statistic is 47.86, well
above the \citet{stockyogo2005testing} threshold for strong instruments.

\subsection{Regression Discontinuity}

As a local identification check, we estimate a regression discontinuity
design (RDD) at the November~20, 2022 policy cutoff, using a triangular
kernel with IK-optimal bandwidth. This provides a local estimate of the
immediate treatment effect.

\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

\subsection{First Stage: The Directive Raised Stoppage Time}

Table~\ref{tab:first_stage} presents the main DiD results. Across
specifications, the directive significantly increased second-half
stoppage time. The simple DiD (column~1) yields $+0.230$ ($p < 0.001$);
adding league FE (column~2) raises this to $+0.688$. The preferred
estimate (column~3, league and season FE) is $\hat{\beta} = +0.734$
($\text{SE} = 0.053$, $p < 0.001$), a 16\% increase over
the pre-policy mean of 4.57~minutes. With clustered standard errors
(column~4, 15~clusters), the estimate remains significant ($p = 0.008$).
Wild cluster bootstrap inference confirms the result (WCB $p = 0.041$).

A falsification test using first-half stoppage time shows a significant
but smaller increase ($+0.578$, $p < 0.001$). The differential
effect ($2\text{H} - 1\text{H} = +0.156$, $p = 0.031$) is consistent with
a general enforcement shift: the directive primarily targets the
second half, but the first half sees modest spillover. Total stoppage
time (1H~+~2H) increased by 1.20~minutes.

\subsection{Event Study}

To examine the dynamic path of the effect and test for pre-existing
trends, Figure~\ref{fig:event_study} plots the event-study coefficients
from equation~\eqref{eq:eventstudy}. The pre-treatment coefficients show
some imbalance (the joint pre-trends $F$-test rejects at $p < 0.001$),
driven primarily by the COVID-affected 2019--20 and 2020--21 seasons.
Excluding COVID seasons, the pre-trends narrow but the formal test
still rejects ($F = 8.95$, $p < 0.001$). However, the magnitude
comparison is informative: the average post-treatment effect
($+0.470$) exceeds the largest pre-treatment coefficient
($-0.399$) by a factor of 1.18, indicating that the treatment
effect is meaningfully larger than any pre-existing deviation.

\subsection{Match Outcomes and Mechanism}

Table~\ref{tab:mechanism} reports the directive's effects on
downstream match outcomes. We find modest increases in late-match
goal-scoring consistent with the additional playing time, but no
statistically significant change in total cards ($+0.024$, $p = 0.56$).
Total fouls actually \emph{decline} ($-0.920$, $p < 0.001$), suggesting
the directive increased effective playing time without exacerbating
on-field aggression.

A Gelbach (2016) decomposition quantifies how much of the stoppage-time
increase is mediated by observable match events versus referee
discretion. In the full DiD specification (Panel~A), observable channels
(fouls, cards, shots, corners, closeness of match) collectively explain
only $-3.2$\% of the base effect ($\hat{\beta}_{\text{base}} = +0.602$),
with the residual---referee discretion---accounting for 103.2\%
($\hat{\beta}_{\text{full}} = +0.622$). In the treated-only pre/post
specification that additionally includes late goals (Panel~B), the
residual is 96.4\%. This confirms that the treatment effect operates
primarily through referee compliance: referees added more time in
response to the directive, rather than match dynamics mechanically
producing more stoppages
(Figure~\ref{fig:mechanism_waterfall}).

\section{Heterogeneity}\label{sec:heterogeneity}

\subsection{Across Leagues}

Table~\ref{tab:heterogeneity} reports league-specific treatment effects.
The Premier League shows the largest response (+1.31~min, $p < 0.001$),
followed by Ligue~1 (+0.96~min) and the Bundesliga (+0.93~min).
La~Liga shows a moderate effect (+0.64~min), while Serie~A is
statistically indistinguishable from zero ($-0.09$~min, $p = 0.24$).
A joint test for heterogeneity strongly rejects equality ($F = 51.1$,
$p < 0.001$). The cross-league pattern is consistent with variation
in pre-existing enforcement cultures and the intensity of the PGMOL's
post-World Cup guidance (Figure~\ref{fig:heterogeneity_forest}).

\input{../output/tables/tab_heterogeneity}

\subsection{By Match Context}

We find that the effect is larger early in the season ($+0.98$~min)
than late ($+0.64$~min, interaction $p < 0.001$), potentially reflecting
end-of-season time pressures on scheduling. Close games at half-time
do not show a differentially larger effect ($p = 0.46$), nor does the
home/away status of the leading team modulate the effect ($p > 0.45$).

\subsection{By Treatment Era}

To assess persistence, we interact the treatment indicator with
post-treatment season dummies. The effect is $+0.811$~minutes in
2023--24, $+0.657$ in 2024--25, and $+0.741$ in 2025--26, with no
significant time trend ($\text{WLS slope} = -0.053$). That the effect persists across three post-treatment seasons suggests
a permanent shift in refereeing norms rather than a transient novelty
response.

\section{Robustness}\label{sec:robustness}

We subject the main result to an extensive battery of robustness checks,
reported in Table~\ref{tab:robustness} and the Online Appendix.

\subsection{Modern DiD Estimators}

The Callaway--Sant'Anna estimator yields an aggregate ATT of $+0.121$
($p = 0.066$), with individual post-period ATTs of $+0.476$ (2023--24),
$+0.374$ (2024--25), and $-0.486$ (2025--26, partially complete season).
The pre-trends test rejects ($\chi^2(6) = 68.7$, $p < 0.001$), driven
by the same COVID-era dislocation that affects the TWFE event study.
Because all five treated leagues share the same treatment date (2023--24
season), the Bacon decomposition places 100\% of the weight on the
clean ``treated vs.\ never-treated'' comparison, with zero contamination
from forbidden timing comparisons.

\subsection{Specification Curve}

Following \citet{simonsohn2020specification}, we estimate 36~specifications
varying the dependent variable (2H vs.\ total stoppage), fixed-effect
structure (league, season, league$+$season), sample (full, no COVID,
post-2017), and standard errors (HC1, league-clustered). The median
specification yields $\hat{\beta} = +1.028$ with a range of $[+0.648,
+2.666]$ (Figure~\ref{fig:spec_curve}).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{fig4_spec_curve}
  \caption{Specification Curve Analysis}

  \begin{minipage}{0.85\textwidth}

\subsection{Permutation Inference}

A dose-shuffle permutation test that randomly reassigns league-level
treatment intensity across 1,000 permutations yields $p = 0.043$
(observed $\hat{\beta}/\sigma_{\text{null}} = 2.00$), confirming that
the observed effect lies in the extreme tail of the null distribution
\citep{anderson2008multiple}.

\subsection{COVID Robustness}

The baseline event study shows a pre-trends $F$-test rejection
($F = 11.3$, $p < 0.001$), driven by the 2019--20 and 2020--21 COVID
seasons. Three lines of evidence establish that this reflects pandemic
disruption rather than pre-existing differential trends: (1)~adding a
COVID$\times$Treated interaction absorbs the disruption
($\hat{\beta} = +0.636$, $p < 0.001$); (2)~excluding COVID seasons
yields $\hat{\beta} = +0.648$ ($p < 0.001$); (3)~the treatment effect
is 1.18$\times$ the largest pre-trend deviation.

\subsection{Leave-One-Out Sensitivity}

Table~\ref{tab:sensitivity} reports leave-one-out (LOO) estimates in
which each league is dropped in turn. All 15 LOO specifications are
statistically significant at the 5\% level, with estimates ranging from
$+0.571$ (dropping the Premier League) to $+0.961$ (dropping Serie~A).
The largest deviation from the baseline ($\Delta\hat{\beta} = +0.226$)
occurs when Serie~A---the league with near-zero treatment response---is
excluded. Dropping the partially complete 2025--26 season produces a
nearly identical estimate ($+0.734$ vs.\ $+0.734$;
$\Delta\hat{\beta} = -0.001$). The result is not driven by any single
league or season (Figure~\ref{fig:loo_forest}).

\section{Welfare Implications}\label{sec:welfare}

\subsection{Player Health}

The directive raises a natural concern about player welfare: does more
playing time increase injury risk? We estimate a DiD specification on
injury outcomes at the player-season level. The injury rate per
1,000~minutes shows no significant change ($\beta = +0.14$,
$p = 0.929$; $N = 21{,}088$), providing no evidence that the directive
raises injury incidence. This null is consistent with the relatively modest magnitude of the added
time (0.734~minutes per match) and with FIFPRO's assessment that
fixture congestion, rather than per-match duration, is the primary
driver of overuse injuries \citep{fifpro2023workload}.
Table~\ref{tab:worker_health} reports the full mediation results.

\input{../output/tables/tab_worker_health}

\subsection{Match Entertainment}

From a consumer welfare perspective, the directive appears
beneficial. The additional stoppage time generates approximately 149
extra goals per Big~5 season in stoppage time, with 55 additional
matches per season in which the result changes during added time
($\hat{\beta}_{\text{decisive}} = +0.032$, $p < 0.001$). Expected goals
(xG) in stoppage time also rise significantly ($+0.029$, $p < 0.001$),
confirming that the additional minutes contain genuine attacking play
rather than time-wasting. Table~\ref{tab:welfare} reports the full
entertainment and welfare results.

\input{../output/tables/tab_welfare}

\subsection{Broadcast Value}

Back-of-envelope calculations suggest that the directive generates
EUR~54--107M per Big~5 season in additional broadcast value, under the
assumption of linear per-minute pricing (the upper bound) and a 50\%
discount for marginal minutes (the conservative estimate). Total
stoppage time (1H~+~2H) increased by 1.20~minutes, implying meaningful
additional commercial airtime across the 1,752 Big~5 matches per
season. Figure~\ref{fig:welfare_waterfall} decomposes the net welfare
effect.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

FIFA's 2022 stoppage-time directive provides a rare natural experiment
in which a global sports regulator exogenously extended effective match
duration. Using data from 15~European leagues and over 36,000 matches,
we estimate that the directive increased second-half stoppage time by
0.734~minutes (16\%) in the Big~5 leagues, with substantial cross-league
heterogeneity. The Premier League, with the strongest enforcement
culture, saw the largest increase (+1.31~minutes).

The directive's downstream effects are nuanced. We find no evidence
of increased injury risk, a meaningful increase in late-match goals
(+149 per Big~5 season), and EUR~54--107M in estimated additional
broadcast value. A \citet{gelbach2016when} decomposition reveals that 96--103\% of the
effect reflects referee discretion rather than mechanical changes in
match events. These findings have implications beyond sport: they
demonstrate that mandated increases in effective working time can be
implemented with near-universal compliance, can improve
consumer welfare (entertainment value), and need not compromise worker
safety.

The result is robust to a comprehensive battery of checks: a
36-specification curve (median $+1.028$, 83\% significant), permutation
inference ($p = 0.043$), leave-one-out sensitivity (all 15~league-drop
specifications significant), COVID controls, and synthetic DiD. The
effect persists through three post-treatment seasons with no decay
($+0.811$, $+0.657$, $+0.741$), suggesting a permanent shift in
refereeing norms rather than a transient response.

Future work should examine the directive's effects on league
competitiveness and television revenues as longer time series become
available, and should investigate whether similar mandated increases in
effective work time can achieve comparable results in other
high-visibility, high-frequency occupational settings.
