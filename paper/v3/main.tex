\begin{abstract}
\noindent
In November 2022, FIFA instructed match officials to fully compensate
for time lost during professional football matches---enforcing a rule
systematically under-applied for decades. We study this enforcement
shift across 15~European leagues and 36,000~matches. The directive
triggered a near-universal norm shift: enforcement intensity tracked
institutional proximity to FIFA, producing a dose-response gradient
from the Premier League ($d = 1.13$) to Serie~B ($d \approx 0$).
The most striking consequence is goal \textit{displacement}: total
goals per match were unchanged ($\beta = -0.033$, $p = 0.39$), but
goals migrated from the final minutes of regulation into expanded
stoppage time, raising the share of matches with a result-changing
stoppage-time goal from 7.3\% to 10.3\%. A simple model of optimal
attacking intensity under known remaining time rationalises this
pattern. Compliance was near-universal (95\% of referees individually
increased stoppage time), achieved through focal-point coordination
rather than monitoring or sanctions.

\medskip
\noindent

\textbf{Keywords:} goal displacement, stoppage time, focal-point
coordination, norm diffusion, sports economics
\end{abstract}
