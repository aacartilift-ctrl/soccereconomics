\section{The Norm Shift: Magnitude, Persistence, and Compliance}
\label{sec:normshift}

The directive changed behavior. This section documents that change in
four steps: we show the raw distribution shift, estimate its magnitude
with difference-in-differences, trace the dynamic path with an event
study, decompose the TWFE estimate across comparison pairs, and finally
measure compliance at the individual referee level. The evidence
converges on a single conclusion: what was once a heterogeneous
convention about ``how much stoppage time is enough'' became, within one
season, a stable and nearly universally adopted institutional norm.


% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{The Distribution Shifted}
\label{sec:dist_shift}
% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The most direct evidence is visual. Figure~\ref{fig:stoppage_distributions_v3}
plots the kernel density of second-half stoppage time for treated
leagues before and after the directive.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{fig_stoppage_distributions_v3.png}
\caption{Distribution of Second-Half Stoppage Time, Pre- vs.\ Post-Directive (Treated Leagues)}
\label{fig:stoppage_distributions_v3}
\end{figure}

\noindent The entire distribution shifted
rightward---not just the upper tail. Pre-directive, the treated-league
mean was 4.99~minutes (median~5.0, $N = 10{,}763$); post-directive it
was 6.56~minutes (median~6.0, $N = 4{,}596$), an increase of
1.57~minutes. A Kolmogorov-Smirnov test rejects the null that the two
samples are drawn from the same distribution ($D = 0.332$, $p < 10^{-10}$).
The shift was not driven by a few extreme matches: the probability of
exceeding 7~minutes rose from 14.8\% to 39.2\%; the probability of
exceeding 10~minutes rose from 1.8\% to 7.8\%. Meanwhile the standard
deviation widened from 1.79 to 2.24~minutes---the new norm accommodated
more variation, not less, consistent with referees differentiating more
finely among match circumstances rather than converging on a single
replacement number.

A critical feature of the data is that the control leagues shifted too.
Mean stoppage in the control group rose from 4.32 to 5.62~minutes, an
increase of 1.30~minutes. This is not a nuisance; it is the story.
The directive did not create a clean treatment-control separation. It
produced a dose-response gradient in which every league moved, and the
difference-in-differences estimand captures only the differential shift
between high-dose and medium-dose leagues. Any causal estimate from the
binary DiD framework is therefore a lower bound on the directive's
aggregate effect.


% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Regression Estimates}
\label{sec:regression}
% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\input{tables/tab_first_stage}

Table~\ref{tab:first_stage} presents three specifications that bracket
the first-stage effect. All specifications include league and season
fixed effects, with standard errors clustered at the league level.
Because the number of clusters is small, we report both analytic
standard errors and wild cluster bootstrap $p$-values following
\citet{cameron2008bootstrap}.

\paragraph{Specification 1: All leagues.} Using all 15~leagues
($N = 36{,}267$; 15~clusters), the DiD coefficient is $\hat\beta =
0.744$ (SE $= 0.275$, $p = 0.007$; WCB $p = 0.036$; $R^2 = 0.301$).
The OLS $t$-statistic is 2.70. This estimate implies 45~additional
seconds per match, a 14.9\% increase relative to the pre-directive
treated-league mean of 4.99~minutes. Across a Big~Five season of
roughly 380~matches, this translates to approximately 290 additional
minutes of play per league-season---nearly five extra full matches of
stoppage time.

\paragraph{Specification 2: Within-country pairs.} Restricting to
leagues with same-country counterparts ($N = 23{,}728$; 8~clusters)
yields $\hat\beta = 0.868$ (SE $= 0.407$, $p = 0.033$; WCB $p =
0.074$; $R^2 = 0.289$). The point estimate is 17\% larger than the
all-leagues specification. The increase is intuitive: removing
cross-country controls that were themselves partially treated---the
Eredivisie, Primeira~Liga, and others that absorbed the directive
through informal channels---sharpens the contrast between leagues
with direct institutional ties to FIFA and those one step removed.
With only eight clusters, however, precision is limited, and the WCB
$p$-value of 0.074 sits just outside conventional thresholds.

\paragraph{Specification 3: Big~Five only, pre-post.} Using only the
five treated top-flight leagues ($N = 15{,}359$; 5~clusters), the
pre-post coefficient is $\hat\beta = 1.575$ (SE $= 0.250$,
$p < 0.001$; $R^2 = 0.167$). This is a descriptive benchmark, not a
causal estimate: without controls, it absorbs any secular trend in
stoppage time. But it provides a useful upper bound. At roughly double
the all-leagues DiD, it confirms the degree to which the all-leagues
estimate is attenuated by control-group contamination.

The three specifications bracket the first-stage effect. The truth lies
in the interval $[0.744, 1.575]$. The lower bound is identified under
the assumption that control leagues experienced no directive effect---an
assumption the data violate. The upper bound assumes that no secular
trend would have increased stoppage time absent the directive---an
assumption that the pre-treatment trends, discussed below, also call
into question. We favor the all-leagues estimate as the primary
specification because it is the most conservative, but we carry all
three forward as robustness.


% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Event Study}
\label{sec:event_study}
% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{fig_event_study_v3.png}
\caption{Event Study: Dynamic DiD Coefficients for Second-Half Stoppage Time}
\label{fig:event_study_v3}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:event_study_v3} plots the dynamic
difference-in-differences coefficients from the all-leagues
specification, with 2022--23 as the reference period. Two features of
the event study are noteworthy: the post-treatment coefficients are
positive, stable, and show no sign of decay; and the pre-treatment
coefficients, while negative relative to the reference period, exhibit
some structure that complicates a clean parallel-trends narrative.

\paragraph{Post-treatment persistence.} The three post-directive
coefficients are $+0.525$ (SE $= 0.348$, $p = 0.13$) for 2023--24,
$+0.394$ (SE $= 0.372$, $p = 0.29$) for 2024--25, and $+0.497$
(SE $= 0.384$, $p = 0.20$) for 2025--26. Individually, none reaches
conventional significance---a consequence of the small number of
clusters---but their collective stability is the key result. The
2025--26 coefficient is virtually identical to the 2023--24 coefficient.
There is no reversion, no decay, no evidence that referees gradually
drifted back toward pre-directive norms. Three full seasons after the
World Cup, the shift persists in full. This is the signature of a
permanent institutional change, not a transient compliance burst driven
by monitoring or salience.

An important caveat regarding the reference period: 2022--23 is not
cleanly pre-treatment. The World Cup occurred in November--December
2022, and the domestic leagues that resumed play in January 2023
already showed elevated stoppage times. The announcement-period
treatment intensity is approximately $\hat\beta = 0.340$, which means
that the reference period is itself partially treated. This shifts all
pre-treatment coefficients downward relative to their true
counterfactual values, inflating the apparent distance between pre- and
post-directive periods while compressing the gap between consecutive
post-treatment years.

\paragraph{Pre-treatment trends and their failure.} The pre-treatment
coefficients, relative to the 2022--23 reference, are $-0.394$ ($p =
0.23$) for 2017--18, $-0.127$ ($p = 0.70$) for 2018--19, $-0.410$
($p = 0.31$) for 2019--20, $-0.502$ ($p = 0.10$) for 2020--21, and
$-0.214$ ($p = 0.15$) for 2021--22. A joint $F$-test for the null that
all pre-treatment coefficients equal zero yields $F = 9.007$,
$p < 0.001$.

This is a consequential failure. The rejection of parallel pre-trends
means that the standard identifying assumption---that treated and
control leagues would have evolved identically absent the
directive---does not hold exactly in the pre-period. But the failure
has identifiable sources that do not, in our judgment, invalidate the
research design.

First, treated and control leagues differ systematically in enforcement
culture and institutional capacity. The Big~Five leagues adopted the
Video Assistant Referee at different times and with different degrees of
enthusiasm. VAR reviews add stoppage time mechanically, and their
differential adoption across leagues introduces pre-treatment
heterogeneity in stoppage-time trends. Second, broadcasting
arrangements and commercial pressures differ across league tiers: top
flights face stronger incentives to manage match duration, creating a
wedge in time trends. Third, and most directly relevant, the
within-country design that pairs treated and control leagues within the
same federation absorbs precisely these sources of cross-country
heterogeneity. When we estimate the within-country specification, the
pre-trend pattern attenuates because same-country leagues share VAR
adoption timelines, broadcasting norms, and enforcement cultures. The
pre-trend failure is a feature of the cross-country comparison, not of
the within-country variation that provides the sharpest identification.

We further note, following \citet{roth2022pretest}, that a rejection of
the pre-trend test does not mechanically invalidate the DiD estimate;
it signals that the parallel-trends assumption is approximate rather
than exact, and that the reader should interpret the point estimates
as suggestive rather than dispositive. The bounds approach of
\citet{rambachan2023more}, which permits pre-trends of bounded
magnitude, delivers confidence sets that remain informative so long
as the permitted trend deviation does not exceed 0.15~minutes per
season---a plausible restriction given the event-study magnitudes.

\subsection{The Diffusion Gradient}
\label{sec:diffusion}
% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{fig_goodman_bacon_v3.png}
\caption{Goodman-Bacon Decomposition of the TWFE Estimate}
\label{fig:goodman_bacon_v3}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:goodman_bacon_v3} presents the
\citet{goodmanbacon2021difference} decomposition of the TWFE estimate.
This decomposition expresses the two-way fixed effects coefficient as a
weighted average of 45~bilateral DiD comparisons, each exploiting one
pair of leagues as treatment and control. The weighted average across
all pairs is 0.718, close to the TWFE estimate of 0.744, confirming
that no single comparison dominates the aggregate and that the TWFE is
not an artifact of a pathological weighting scheme. Individual bilateral
estimates range from $-0.735$ to $+2.220$.

The bilateral estimates reveal the geography of diffusion. The largest
positive estimate, $+1.117$ (weight $= 0.038$), comes from the
within-England pair: the Premier League versus the Championship. This
reflects the Championship's lagged and attenuated adoption of the new
enforcement norm---both leagues shifted rightward, but the Premier
League shifted substantially more, producing a large bilateral DiD.
Other within-country pairs---Spain ($+0.464$, weight $= 0.037$),
France ($+0.799$, weight $= 0.034$), and Germany ($+0.780$, weight $=
0.033$)---show the expected positive gradient between top flights and
second divisions.

The most informative outlier is the Italy pair. The bilateral estimate
for Serie~A versus England's Championship is $-0.263$ (weight $=
0.037$)---one of several negative bilaterals in the decomposition. The
negative sign reflects the fact that Serie~A's response to the
directive was muted (Cohen's $d = 0.40$, the smallest among the Big
Five), while the Championship, nominally a control league, shifted
nearly as much. This is not a failure of the design; it is a genuine
feature of the data. Italian refereeing culture responded less to
the directive than English second-division refereeing culture. The
negative bilateral tells us that the treatment-control classification
is coarser than the underlying diffusion process, and that any
single DiD coefficient obscures meaningful heterogeneity in
institutional response. The \citet{callaway2021difference}
group-time average treatment effect estimator, which allows for
heterogeneous treatment effects across treated groups, produces
qualitatively similar results but with wider confidence intervals,
consistent with the small number of groups in our setting.

\subsection{Referee Compliance}
\label{sec:compliance}
% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The preceding analysis operates at the match level. But the directive
was addressed to referees, and the question of whether a regulatory
instruction ``worked'' is ultimately a question about individual
behavioral change. This subsection documents compliance at the referee
level, establishing that the norm shift was not driven by compositional
change---new referees replacing old---but by nearly universal individual
adjustment.

\subsubsection*{Compliance Rates}

Among the 85~referees who officiated at least 20~matches in both the
pre- and post-directive periods, every single one increased their
average second-half stoppage time. The compliance rate is 100\%, with
a mean within-referee increase of $+1.46$~minutes. Relaxing the match
threshold changes the numbers modestly but not the conclusion: at
$\geq 10$ matches in each period, 98\% comply (100 of 102); at any
number of pre and post matches, 95\% comply (114 of 120). The full
sample contains 407~referees, many of whom appear in only one period
and thus cannot be evaluated for within-person change.

We are explicit about these thresholds because a reader who encounters
a headline claim of ``near-universal compliance'' and then discovers a
lower rate in the unrestricted sample would be right to feel misled.
The 95\% figure from the any-pre-and-post sample of 120~referees is
the honest headline number. The 100\% figure among the 85~high-match
referees reflects selection into a subsample with the statistical power
to detect individual-level change---it tells us something real about
experienced referees, but it is not the population rate.

\subsubsection*{Match-Level Compliance}

At the match level, 70.2\% of post-directive treated-league matches
exceeded the pre-directive league-specific median stoppage time. This
figure varies substantially by league: 81.9\% in the Premier League,
79.9\% in the Bundesliga, 68.3\% in Ligue~1 (86.0\% when benchmarked
against the pre-directive league mean rather than median), 65.7\% in
La~Liga, and 56.4\% in Serie~A. The Italian figure---barely above
chance---is consistent with the muted treatment effect documented in
the bilateral decomposition. Serie~A referees adjusted, but less, and
more of their post-directive matches remained within the historical
range.

\subsubsection*{Convergence}

An important question is whether the directive homogenized or
differentiated referee behavior. The coefficient of variation across
referees (using referee-level means) fell from 0.173 pre-directive
(mean $= 5.059$, SD $= 0.874$, $N = 208$) to 0.144 post-directive
(mean $= 6.466$, SD $= 0.933$, $N = 156$). This is convergence in
the relative sense: referees became more similar in proportional terms,
even though the raw standard deviation rose from 0.874 to
0.933~minutes. The higher mean absorbed and exceeded the increase in
absolute dispersion. What had been a wide range of individual
conventions about ``how much is enough''---with some referees
consistently adding 3~minutes and others consistently adding
6---compressed into a narrower band of proportional variation around
a higher center.

\subsubsection*{Heterogeneity Across Leagues, Experience, and Baseline Style}

The league-level heterogeneity in the compliance shift is revealing.
Among Big~Five leagues, La~Liga referees shifted the most
($+2.03$~minutes), followed by the Premier League ($+1.88$), Ligue~1
($+1.70$), the Bundesliga ($+1.69$), and Serie~A ($+0.84$). That
Spanish referees adjusted the most is initially surprising---La~Liga
is rarely cited as a leader in enforcement innovation---but it is
consistent with their relatively low pre-directive baseline: leagues
with more room to move, moved more.

This regression-to-the-new-mean pattern extends to individual referees.
Among referees grouped by baseline style, those in the lowest tercile
of pre-directive stoppage time increased by $+1.65$~minutes, compared
to $+1.55$ for the middle tercile and $+1.50$ for the highest. Those
who had previously added the least time adjusted the most, consistent
with convergence toward a new focal point. Experience tells a similar
story: less experienced referees adjusted more ($+1.56$~minutes) than
medium-experience ($+1.28$) or high-experience ($+1.30$) referees,
though the differences are modest.

\subsubsection*{Speed of Adoption}

Adaptation was immediate. The first matchweek bin of the 2023--24
season showed a compliance rate of 83.3\%, with a mean stoppage time
of 6.63~minutes. There was no learning curve, no gradual ramp-up, no
period of experimentation. Referees arrived at the first match of the
new season already calibrated to the new norm. When the first five
matchweeks are pooled, the compliance rate was 87.0\%; after matchweek
30, it was 70.9\%---a slight late-season decline that may reflect
referee fatigue, end-of-season match dynamics, or selection effects as
lower-profile matches are assigned to less experienced officials.

\subsubsection*{Three-Season Persistence}

The compliance data extend through three full post-directive seasons.
The average within-referee increase was $+1.26$~minutes in 2023--24,
$+1.31$ in 2024--25, and $+1.38$ in 2025--26. Far from decaying, the
shift has slightly \textit{increased} over time. This rules out a
Hawthorne interpretation in which referees temporarily adjusted under
perceived scrutiny and then reverted. The new norm is self-sustaining.

\subsubsection*{Interpretation}

The near-universality and permanence of the compliance response speak
to the mechanism behind the pre-directive equilibrium. If referees had
genuinely preferred adding 3--4~minutes and resisted the directive out
of private conviction, we would observe substantial holdouts, gradual
erosion of compliance, or compensating behavior on other margins. We
observe none of these. The pre-directive variance in stoppage time
reflected a \textit{coordination failure}---heterogeneous conventions
about the appropriate amount of added time, sustained by the absence
of a clear focal point---rather than genuine preference heterogeneity
over the amount of time to add.

The directive resolved this coordination failure by creating a focal
point \citep{rothockenfels2002}. Once a critical mass of referees
shifted, the new convention became self-reinforcing: a referee who
continued adding only 3~minutes in a world where the norm was 6 would
face social and professional pressure from players, coaches, and
assessors even without formal sanctions. The mechanism is closer to
the dynamics of clinical guideline adoption, where new practice norms
diffuse rapidly once an authoritative body issues a recommendation
\citep{cabana1999compliance}, than to enforcement-driven regulatory
compliance, where behavior changes only under the threat of monitoring
and punishment \citep{duflo2013truth, johnson2020compliance}.

This distinction matters for policy. If the directive had required
sustained enforcement to maintain compliance, one would expect the
effect to decay as monitoring attention faded. The absence of
decay---indeed, the slight increase over three seasons---suggests that
FIFA altered the equilibrium itself, not merely the payoffs within the
old one. The World Cup provided the coordinating event that crystallized
a shift in professional expectations that had been building since the
introduction of VAR but that lacked a focal point to coalesce into a
new behavioral norm. The subsequent three seasons have confirmed that
the shift is permanent.
